Oceanography, Spring 2021

Introduction

The Problem: Sea Level Rise

Since 1900, climate-induced sea-level rise has heightend global waters approximately 8 inches, inducing increased storm intensities and and wide scale flooding in low-lying communities. Modern projections place another 12-inches of sea-level rise above the year 2000’s global sea averages. By 2100, we could see the oceans rise up to 2.5 meters. Such a rise would destroy low-altitude urban environments causing immesurable economic, cultural, and environmental damage.

Many communities have already borne witness to the devestating impacts of sea-level rise. Climatic events are only expected to increase in size and frequency, meaning we must begin preparing our communities for rising tides today. Hence, this City Planner's Introductory Guide to Sea Level Rise was developed to assist those developing our cities begin their research in how to prepare their communities.

The Interim Solution: Accomodation & Costal Defense

Accomodation and Costal Defenses refer to the necessary steps we must take to greenify / weatherize our communities and protect our coastlines, respectfully. We refer to these steps as interim solutions because they do not soley protect us from the far-reaching impacts of climate change and sea-level rise. Rather, they are practices we must incorporate into our larger struggle to stop the progress of climate change and reach a carbon nuetral society.

This guide is meant to serve as a city planner's introductory step for his or her research into sustainable infrastrucutre. Every community is unique and responsible for its own financial, cultural, and envrionmental security. As such, each city planner may use this introductory information (and attached readings - hyperlinked to the numbered circles below each section) to further his or her own reasearch in meeting the needs of their individual communities.

Adaptive Design

Case Study: The Afsluitdijk

When developing sustainable infrastructure, it is important to consider its long-term flexibility in an unpredictable climate. An inflexible, static structure that alleviates tidal pressures today may be detrimental in the long-term. A permament sea-wall, for instance, may block crashing waves today, but it may fail in twenty years when the average sea-level has risen and storm intensity has increased. Instead, it vital to have infrastrucutre that can be expanded upon. If the sea-wall can be heightened, your community can take appopriate action. If the sea-wall is immutable, your community will be forced to expend the resources to replace it entirely or suffer the infrastrucutre's shortcomings.

The Netherlands, which has about a third of its country below sea level, has developed incredible flood control infrastrucutre using adaptive designs. The Afsluitdijk, for instance, servers as a non-static, 20-mile long dam protecting Amsterdamn and the surrounding country side from storm surges originating in the North Sea. The dam was origionally constructed in 1932 and sucessfully kept Amsterdam's port above local sea-levels. However, as the North Sea rose, pump stations and aditional land had to be reserved to protect the country from intensifying storm surges. Now, the damn in undergoing a multi-year construction project to increase it's size, add storm-breakers, and more. The Afsluitdijk is also being updated to include fish passages, renewable energy testing sites, and a safer, updated highway. From its inital contruction, the Dutch knew their designs would require modifications to meet changing demands. There assumption paid off and now international communities should look to their work for inspiration on how to build their own sustainable infrastructure.

City Planning

Sustainable Infrastructure: Green and Weatherized

As storms intensify and sea-levels rise, we need to make sure our low-lying infrastrucutre is prepared for flooding and hurricane-like events. Urban and developed areas retain water easily. Unlike rural areas which allow for runoff, urban centers have concreate, pavement, and other building materials that prevent water from dispersing evenly across the land. Instead, water is unable to permeate these building materials which can rapidly lead to flooding.
